# Scenario Craft

Distilled from the best published design writing (The Alexandrian, GUMSHOE/Robin
Laws, Sly Flourish, Sean McCoy's Mothership layout school, convention-GM guides
by Katrina Ostrander and Burn After Running). These are the field's proven rules;
the house structure in `convention-one-shot.md` already embodies most of them —
use this file when making design *decisions* or debugging a draft that isn't
working.

## Situations, not plots (The Alexandrian)

- Prep **situations** (locations, NPCs with goals and resources, available
  information), never event chains — a scripted plot is a chain of potential
  points of failure.
- Write **goal-oriented opponents**: what the antagonist is trying to do, on
  what timetable, with what tools (squads, safehouses, informants). Improvise
  their reactions; never script them.
- Always write the **adversary timeline**: what happens if the PCs do nothing.
  (House form: The Countdown.)

## Clues and mystery (Three Clue Rule + GUMSHOE)

- The Alexandrian's Three Clue Rule (three independent routes to every
  necessary conclusion) is implemented as the **house standard** in
  `convention-one-shot.md`: automatic-on-right-approach + written fallback +
  Countdown event. Apply that standard; this section explains the why.
- **Never gate progress behind a die roll to notice something.** Finding core
  clues is automatic for the right approach in the right scene; rolls buy
  *extra* information. The game is *interpreting* clues, not finding them.
- A **core clue** points to another scene. Test: if it's not worth writing in a
  player's notes, it isn't a core clue. Write each scene with lead-ins and
  lead-outs.
- **Don't author red herrings** — players manufacture their own false leads for
  free. Over-provision true information instead; the sorting is the gameplay.
- Design **backwards from the truth**: write what actually happened and what
  the villain does next, then lay the trail from the trigger event.
- Keep a **revelation checklist** (the 8–12 things players must eventually
  learn), abstracted from where they're discovered — any scene can deliver a
  homeless revelation. Carry undiscovered ones forward. (Sly Flourish's
  "secrets and clues".)
- Unstick tools, in order: fallback delivery of the missed clue → **proactive
  antagonist event** (a new death, the opposition strikes) → invent a new core
  clue on the spot. Structures serve the table, not vice versa.

## Pacing (convention wisdom)

- **One substantive scene per hour of real play**; a 3h30 slot holds ~5. The
  critical path should fill only about *half* the slot — deliberation, rules,
  and jokes eat the rest. (Total planned content ≈ 3:15 against the 3:30
  budget, per the house cut discipline.)
- **Dice roll within the first 20 minutes.** Open in motion (in medias res or
  pressure-adjacent) and let introductions surface during the action, not as
  round-the-table monologues.
- Premise formula for pitches: "When [disruptive event], will [adjective]
  [group] manage to [verb] the [thing]?"
- Introduce mechanics when first needed, not in a front-loaded rules lecture.
- Spotlight is a resource: track who hasn't had a meaningful roll or scene and
  aim the next beat at them.
- Personal Agendas (Free League cinematic model): per-act secret goals on the
  pregens are a drama engine — quiet tension in Acts One–Two, open
  confrontation fuel in Act Three. The house "Private box" is the single-arc
  version; use per-act agendas when the scenario needs PvP-adjacent heat.

## Structural skeletons worth stealing

- **Five Room Dungeon** (any genre): Guardian/Entrance → Puzzle or Social →
  Trick/Setback → Climax → Revelation/Reward. Good emergency skeleton for a
  short slot or a side episode.
- **Fronts** and **progress clocks** (see `campaign.md`): the Countdown is the
  wall-clock variant; use segment clocks when antagonist triggers are
  event-driven rather than time-driven.

## Writing for the running GM (McCoy / OSR layout school)

- **The cascade:** can this prose be bullets? the bullets a table? the table a
  diagram? Always compress downward.
- Tell the GM **the truth on page one** — mystery is for players, never for the
  reader. (House form: Plot Summary before any scene.)
- Read-aloud text: players disengage after ~two sentences. Keep boxed text
  short and sensory, or replace with a bullet list of details the GM voices in
  their own words.
- No mid-scene page-flips: stat lines live next to their encounter, maps next
  to their key, cross-references carry explicit pointers.
- Immediate impression first, then interactables, then hidden things — in every
  location key.
- Expressive terseness: telegram-minimal strips usable flavour; purple prose
  buries the one fact that matters in paragraph three. One evocative detail per
  element, load-bearing words in bold.
