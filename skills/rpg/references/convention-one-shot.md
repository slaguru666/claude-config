# Convention One-Shot Structure

The house format for 4-hour convention slots. This structure is proven across
Continuum, Contingency, ChaosiumCon and Airecon games — deviate only with a
stated reason in the Design intent.

## Master Rules

### Structure
- **Introduction → Act One → Act Two → Act Three → Epilogue**
- Maximum runtime **3h30 (210 min) of play** inside a 4-hour slot, plus a
  10-minute break — ~3h40 wall clock, leaving real margin: strangers at a con
  table run slower than any playtest.
- Budgets: Intro 15 · Act One 60 · Act Two 90 · Act Three 30 · Epilogue 15.
  These are **ceilings, not fills** — plan ~3:15 of actual content against
  them. A scenario that playtests "on schedule with zero slack" is over-stuffed.
- **The session clock counts play time.** Pause it during the break; every
  cumulative time and checkpoint in the scenario is on that clock. (The GM
  console's clock supports this.)
- **Act Three always happens.** Compress Act Two if needed; never sacrifice the
  climax. Hard rule: at 2:45 on the clock, go to Act Three regardless.
- Every act ends with a **Pacing Note**: what must be true before the next act,
  and a specific fast-track action if the clock has beaten the table.
- Place the 10-minute **break** at the end of Act One, on a natural cliffhanger.
- A one-shot must **end written**: closure or *designed* ambiguity, never an
  unwritten "to be continued". The Epilogue includes an outcomes table
  (Full / Partial / Failure / Pyrrhic — or scenario-specific named endings)
  and an adaptable closing read-aloud.

### Timing table
Include a Session Timing Guide with a **GM Checkpoint** column — a one-line
test the GM can apply mid-session ("both copies met, chase done, deadline
known"). Checkpoints are what make the timing table usable in play.

### Clues — the house standard
Every essential clue has **three routes** to the players:
1. **Automatic on the right approach** — the right skill or question in the
   right scene finds it without a roll; rolls only buy *extra* information.
2. **A written fallback** — NPC volunteers it, environmental prompt, second
   location.
3. **The Countdown** — a proactive antagonist move that delivers or forces the
   revelation (this is the universal third route; it's why the Countdown table
   is mandatory).

Plus:
- Clues form a **guided trail** — each points to the next, but players should
  feel they discovered it, not followed a map.
- Every act: at least one **essential** clue and one **optional** clue
  (optional = reward for thorough play; trail survives without it).
- Essential clues never live behind a search roll or in a location's "Hidden"
  line — Hidden is for optional/extra material only.
- Clue trail is a table per act: # | Clue | Location/Source | How Found |
  What It Reveals | Type. Number clues continuously across acts, one fallback
  line per essential clue.
- Keep a **Revelations checklist** (8–12 one-liners, abstracted from where
  they're found) after the Plot Summary. Anything undelivered by the end of
  Act Two is folded into Act Three's confirmation scene or consciously
  dropped — the Act Two Pacing Note says which. Beyond that, Act Three
  contains **no new information** — only confirmation and consequence.

### The countdown clock
Antagonists and world events move on schedule whether or not players act.
Write a timetable ("The Countdown") the GM advances when the party stalls —
and make each advance *felt* (a glimpsed van, a witness gone quiet). Keep the
opposition **one step behind the players until the climax** — pressure, not
punishment.

### Design intent block
Right after the Overview, 3–5 numbered statements, e.g. "Investigation-first:
no required combat", "Built to split: Acts One and Two run as 2+2 parallel
tracks", "Ambiguous ending: conversation, not firefight". This is the contract
the rest of the document must honour, and the first thing a reviewer checks.

### Plot Summary + Player Experience
Two views of the same scenario, both mandatory:
- **Plot Summary (GM truth)** — what actually happened/is happening,
  player-independent, with a dated timeline. Players never see this page.
- **Player Experience** — one paragraph of the scenario as lived from the
  table, second person, spoiler-shaped but honest. This is the pitch — if it
  reads flat, the scenario is flat.

## Slot logistics
- Default **4 players** with pregens (6 max for some systems — pick per game
  and say so). The Overview states the scaling line: which player counts the
  scenario runs at, which pregen drops first at fewer, how splits adjust.
  Nothing essential may live *only* on one PC.
- **Safety tools, stated in 30 seconds, no ceremony** (X-card or open door —
  UK cons expect it, and scenarios with private seams that cut close need the
  exit ramp). One line in the Introduction covers it.
- **Slot Zero** (the first 15 minutes, before the fiction): seating + safety
  line → pitch each pregen in one spoken tagline (the sheet's italic essence
  line) → players choose or GM deals in ≤5 minutes → open in motion.
- **Party splits** are a feature: parallel scene tracks (Track A / Track B)
  with engineered cross-team payoffs — each half holds half of a revelation.
  Alternate between tables in ~12-minute segments, ending each segment on a
  pull ("what was the hurry?").
- One **set-piece** per scenario (chase, heist, séance...) with its own GM
  quick-reference page. During set-pieces, give idle players jobs: hand them
  the obstacle cards, let them voice the bystanders.
- **Content warnings** in the Overview. Conventions require them and players
  deserve them.

## Endings
- Aim the climax at a **decision, not a die roll**. The best Act Three is a
  situation where every choice costs something and the scenario honours all
  of them ("nobody gets to be right; the players decide who pays").
- Write an "If someone shoots" (or equivalent chaos) contingency — the table
  that torches the negotiation must also get a real ending.
- List **compromise endings** players will invent (partial trades, third
  options). If a playtest finds one, write it in.
- Epilogue delivers per-PC **vignettes** keyed to the outcome, then table
  debrief. Land the last line on the scenario's theme.
- **Early finish** (table cracks it at 2:30): expand the per-PC vignettes,
  then run The Tangent as a bonus scene — it's already written.

## Fitting the slot (cut discipline)
- Plan ~3:15 of content against the 3:30 budget; playtest timing includes
  setup drift.
- Pre-write the cuts: each act's Pacing Note names what to drop first.
- Deploy pressure lines (an NPC checking a watch) at the *first* sign of
  circular table debate, not when the clock is already lost.
