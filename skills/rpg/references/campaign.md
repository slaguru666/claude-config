# Campaign & Multi-Session Material

Tim will say when material is campaign-shaped rather than a convention one-shot.
The GM-readability rules, clue discipline, and production standards all still
apply; what changes is the shape of time.

## What changes from the one-shot format

- **Timing loosens.** No 3h30 hard ceiling, no 2:45 rule. Keep per-session
  targets and checkpoints, but sessions may end mid-arc.
- **Endings invert.** Sessions *should* end on unresolved peril, an escalating
  bang (stakes suddenly transformed), or an earned quiet character beat — the
  cliffhanger types a one-shot must avoid. The *arc* still needs a written
  destination.
- **Hooks must respect choice.** Con players opted in by sitting down; campaign
  players resent coerced hooks. Offer situation-hooks the party can approach
  from any angle, and plant **long-term hooks** whose payoff is sessions away.
- **Foreshadowing is a discipline.** Before writing session N, re-read what is
  planned for N+1..N+3 and seed their elements now. Twists only land if the
  clues predate them.
- **Carry-forward bookkeeping** at the end of each session:
  - Undiscovered revelations roll into the next session's list.
  - Fronts advance a grim portent; clocks tick for everything ignored.
  - Log NPC status, faction posture, promises made, and loose player theories
    (player theories are free plot — steal the good ones).
  - Persist all of this to Graphiti so any machine can pick the campaign up.
- **Structure by fronts, not acts.** Bundle dangers into fronts, each with an
  impending doom and escalating portents. Prep situations per the Alexandrian;
  let acts emerge in play.
- **Characters progress.** Advancement, gear drift, and relationship maps need
  tracking; pregen audit rules from `pregens.md` apply to any handed-out PCs
  but expect player-authored characters here.

## Session prep loop (Sly Flourish's 8 steps, house-adapted)

1. Review the characters (and last session's carry-forward log)
2. Create a strong start — in motion within 20 minutes
3. Outline potential scenes (brief, disposable)
4. List secrets and clues — ~10 one-liners, abstracted from location
5. Develop locations (immediate impression → interactables → hidden)
6. Outline NPCs (one-line essence + want)
7. Choose opposition
8. Select rewards

## Convention scenario ↔ campaign reuse

A convention one-shot can seed a campaign: "The Tangent" section of every
one-shot names the thread a future session would pull. Going the other way,
compressing campaign material into a slot means cutting to one situation, one
set-piece, one decision — and writing a real ending.
